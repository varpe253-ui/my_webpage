What is Mohenjo-daro?

Mohenjo-daro is one of the largest and best-preserved urban settlements of the ancient Indus Valley Civilisation. Built around 2500 BCE, it was once a thriving metropolis home to an estimated 40,000 residents — making it one of the world's earliest great cities, contemporary with the civilisations of ancient Egypt and Mesopotamia.

The name "Mohenjo-daro" is derived from the Sindhi language and means "Mound of the Dead Men." The city lay buried and forgotten for thousands of years until its dramatic rediscovery in 1922 by Indian archaeologist R. D. Banerji, followed by extensive excavations led by Sir John Marshall of the Archaeological Survey of India.

What astonished archaeologists most was the city's extraordinary level of urban planning — a sophistication rarely seen in the ancient world. Mohenjo-daro was no accident of history; it was a deliberately and brilliantly engineered city.

Location & Historical Period

Mohenjo-daro is located in the Larkana District of Sindh province in present-day Pakistan, on the right bank of the Indus River. It sits approximately 510 kilometres northeast of Karachi. The site covers roughly 300 hectares, though much of it remains unexcavated to this day to preserve the fragile remains.

The city flourished during the Mature Harappan phase (c. 2600–1900 BCE). Around 1900 BCE, Mohenjo-daro and many other Indus cities were mysteriously abandoned. Theories for its decline include climate change, shifts in the course of the Indus River, extended droughts, or invasion — though no single explanation is conclusively accepted.

Historical Importance

"Mohenjo-daro is, indeed, one of the most remarkable ancient cities in the world — a planned city with a sophistication that rivals anything known in the ancient Near East." — Sir Mortimer Wheeler, archaeologist

Mohenjo-daro is considered one of the greatest achievements of early human civilisation. Its grid-pattern streets, standardised fired-brick construction, and sophisticated drainage system — with covered sewers running beneath virtually every street — reveal a level of civic engineering unmatched until the Roman Empire, thousands of years later.

The city's Great Bath, a large public pool measuring 12 × 7 metres, is thought to be among the world's earliest examples of a public water structure, possibly used for ritual purification. The citadel mound, granary structures, and assembly halls suggest a well-organised society with institutions for governance, trade, and religion. Importantly, unlike Egyptian or Mesopotamian cities of the same era, Mohenjo-daro shows no evidence of temples or palaces for a god-king — suggesting a more egalitarian social structure.

Fascinating Facts

Urban Marvels

  • The city's streets were laid out in a near-perfect grid — one of the first in history
  • Houses were built with standardised baked bricks of a consistent ratio (1:2:4)
  • Almost every house had its own private bathroom and drainage connection
  • The covered sewage system predates Roman infrastructure by over 2,000 years
  • A sophisticated weight-and-measure system was used for trade across the civilisation

Mysteries & Discoveries

  • The Indus script found on seals here has never been fully deciphered
  • No weapons of war or evidence of a military class have been found
  • The famous "Dancing Girl" bronze figurine was discovered here, circa 2500 BCE
  • Trade links with Mesopotamia are evidenced by artefacts found at the site
  • UNESCO listed the site as endangered in 1996 due to salt erosion
